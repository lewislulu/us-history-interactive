## Background
In 1823, European powers were attempting to reassert control over Latin America's former colonies following the Napoleonic Wars. The Spanish Empire had collapsed, and newly independent Latin American nations faced the threat of European intervention. The United States feared that European powers would reestablish colonial rule in the Western Hemisphere, threatening both emerging American interests and republican ideals.

## Process
On December 2, 1823, President Monroe delivered a bold foreign policy declaration in his annual message to Congress. Secretary of State John Quincy Adams was the principal architect of this policy. Monroe declared that the Western Hemisphere was no longer open to European colonization, and that any European attempt to interfere in American affairs would be viewed as a hostile act toward the United States. In exchange, America promised not to interfere with existing European colonies or European internal affairs.

## Result
The Monroe Doctrine became a cornerstone of American foreign policy, influencing it for two centuries. Though the United States initially lacked the naval power to enforce this declaration, it established the principle of American dominance in the Western Hemisphere. This policy was invoked numerous times throughout history, from blocking European intervention to justifying America's own interventionist policies. It represented America's transition from isolationism to regional hegemony.

**AP Note**: The Monroe Doctrine, drafted primarily by Secretary of State John Quincy Adams, was a unilateral declaration of American foreign policy. It established principles of anti-colonialism and US hemispheric dominance, and though initially lacking enforcement capability, became a guiding principle of American diplomacy for 200 years.
